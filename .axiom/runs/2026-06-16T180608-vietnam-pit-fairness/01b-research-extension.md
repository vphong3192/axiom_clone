## Question A: Expat Exemptions — Fairness Analysis

### A.1 Exact current rules for each exemption

**A.1.1 Relocation allowance**
- [B] One-time (one-off) relocation allowance for foreigners relocating to Vietnam is fully exempt from PIT. Amount is based on whatever is stipulated in the labor contract or employer-employee agreement — no statutory monetary cap is specified in the Circular. Governed by Article 2, Clause 2 of Circular 111/2013/TT-BTC (issued 15 August 2013; effective 1 October 2013). — source: https://english.luatvietnam.vn/circular-no-111-2013-tt-btc-of-august-15-2013-guiding-the-law-on-personal-income-tax-the-law-amending-and-supplementing-a-number-of-articles-of-th-80846-doc1.html (site returned 403 but content confirmed via multiple secondary sources citing the same article)
- [B] The 2026 amended PIT Law (passed 10 December 2025, effective 1 July 2026) retains the relocation exemption for expats. — source: https://kpmg.com/xx/en/our-insights/gms-flash-alert/2026/flash-alert-2026-040.html (search-confirmed, site returned 403); confirmed by https://vietnam.incorp.asia/pit-in-vietnam/ (search result summary)

**A.1.2 Annual home-leave airfare**
- [B] One round-trip airfare per year paid by the employer for a foreign employee on annual leave is PIT-exempt. The ticket must indicate the country where the employee is a national or where the employee's family lives. Governed by Article 2, Clause 2 of Circular 111/2013/TT-BTC. — source: search result summary citing Circular 111/2013, https://www.vietnam-briefing.com/news/personal-income-tax-exemptions-and-reductions.html/
- [B] No equivalent airfare benefit exists for Vietnamese nationals working in Vietnam. This exemption is explicitly framed as applying to foreign employees. — source: ibid.

**A.1.3 Children's school fees at non-Vietnamese curriculum schools**
- [B] Employer-paid tuition for an expat's children studying in Vietnam from kindergarten through high school is PIT-exempt. The exemption applies to general education schools (including non-Vietnamese curriculum / international schools — the exemption is for the employer paying any school in Vietnam K-12). Supporting documents required: invoice from the school and the relevant labor contract. Governed by Article 2, Clause 2 of Circular 111/2013/TT-BTC. — source: https://dhplaw.vn/en/personal-income-tax-is-not-exempt-when-paying-school-fees-for-children-of-vietnamese-working-in-the-country/ (search result confirmed); https://vbp.vn/conditions-record-school-fees-foreign-workers-children-deductible-expenses/?lang=en (site returned 403, content confirmed via search summary)
- [A — key asymmetry confirmed] Vietnamese nationals working domestically do NOT get an equivalent exemption. Under Circular 111/2013/TT-BTC, tuition fees paid by an employer for the children of Vietnamese workers are only PIT-exempt if the Vietnamese worker is posted abroad (and then only for schooling abroad, kindergarten to high school). If a Vietnamese employer pays school fees for a domestically-based Vietnamese worker's children, it is taxable income. A Vietnamese national cannot deduct school fees they pay personally at all. — source: https://dhplaw.vn/en/personal-income-tax-is-not-exempt-when-paying-school-fees-for-children-of-vietnamese-working-in-the-country/ (site returned 403, confirmed via search result snippet which directly quotes the Circular's distinction)

**A.1.4 Housing allowance — 15% cap**
- [B] If an employer provides housing to an employee, the taxable value of the housing benefit is capped at 15% of the employee's total other taxable income (i.e., the housing component cannot represent more than 15% of total taxable employment income, and the excess above that cap is not added to taxable income). This is not a full exemption but a partial cap. Governed by Circular 111/2013/TT-BTC Article 2 (as amended by Circular 92/2015/TT-BTC). — source: search result citing Circular 111/2013 text: "If the amounts paid on behalf of the workers include the house rent, the house rent shall be included in the converted income, but shall not exceed 15% of the total taxable income incurred at the work place." https://globallawexperts.com/comprehensive-tax-compliance-guide-for-foreign-investors-in-vietnam/ (site returned 403, confirmed via search result summary)
- [B] The 15% housing cap applies to all employees (not only expats) where the employer provides housing. However, in practice, employer-provided housing is overwhelmingly an expat benefit; Vietnamese employees almost never receive employer-provided housing as a structured benefit at comparable levels. — source: search result summary, https://vietnam.incorp.asia/pit-in-vietnam/ (search result confirmed)
- [B] The 2026 amended PIT Law retains housing allowance exemptions "under specified conditions." — source: KPMG search result summary, https://kpmg.com/xx/en/our-insights/gms-flash-alert/2026/flash-alert-2026-040.html

### A.2 Equivalent deductions for Vietnamese nationals

- [B] Vietnam PIT provides no deduction for rent paid out-of-pocket by a Vietnamese employee living in rented accommodation. There is no tenant's rent-relief deduction in the system. — source: https://vietanlaw.com/personal-income-tax-deductions-in-2025/ (site returned 403; confirmed by absence in all PwC/KPMG/Vietnam Briefing summaries of allowable deductions)
- [B] Vietnam PIT provides no deduction for school fees or tuition fees paid personally by a Vietnamese employee for their own children (as opposed to the employer-paid school-fee exemption for expats described above). — source: confirmed by the DHP Law article distinction above; also https://vietnam-briefing.com/ search result summaries listing all available deductions, which include no such item
- [B] Vietnam PIT provides no deduction for out-of-pocket medical expenses. The only health-related deduction is the mandatory employee health insurance contribution (1.5% of salary, capped at 20x the statutory minimum wage). — source: https://taxsummaries.pwc.com/vietnam/individual/deductions (site returned 403, confirmed via search result summary listing deductions as: personal allowance, dependent allowance, mandatory insurance contributions, voluntary pension fund up to VND 1M/month, approved charitable donations)
- [B] The full list of deductions available to Vietnamese nationals under PIT 2025 is: (1) personal allowance VND 11M/month (rising to VND 15.5M from 2026); (2) dependent allowance VND 4.4M/dependent/month (rising to VND 6.2M from 2026); (3) mandatory social insurance, health insurance (1.5%), unemployment insurance contributions; (4) voluntary pension fund contributions up to VND 1M/month; (5) approved charitable/humanitarian donations. No rent, no school fees, no medical. — source: https://vietanlaw.com/personal-income-tax-deductions-in-2025/ (site returned 403, confirmed via Vietnam Briefing search result summary and KPMG search result)

### A.3 Policy arguments on discrimination

**Argument that exemptions are NOT discriminatory:**
- [B] The exemptions are formally neutral: they apply to any employer and any employee, regardless of nationality. A Vietnamese national employed by a company that pays for their child's schooling abroad could claim the exemption. The law distinguishes by situation (posted abroad, relocated to Vietnam) not by passport. — source: search result summary from multiple Vietnam tax guides stating "deduction amounts apply uniformly to all tax residents with salary/wage income, regardless of nationality"
- [B] The policy rationale is labor-market necessity: Vietnam needs to attract internationally mobile talent. Relocation costs are genuine one-time extraordinary expenses; home-leave airfare is a necessary cost of cross-border employment; international school fees reflect that expat children cannot attend Vietnamese-curriculum schools. These are not personal lifestyle choices but structural requirements of international employment. — source: https://www.lexology.com/library/detail.aspx?g=15a6745f-5ced-4615-8edf-8b1e4e7a2ce3 (site returned 403, confirmed via search result context)

**Argument that exemptions ARE structurally unequal:**
- [B] In practice, the exemptions are only accessible to employees whose employers structure compensation to include these benefits — a pattern overwhelmingly concentrated among multinational companies employing expats at high salary levels, not among Vietnamese workers earning median wages. A Vietnamese domestic worker paying rent, school fees, or medical costs from post-tax income receives no equivalent relief. — source: synthesized from the DHP Law article (school fee asymmetry confirmed), the housing 15% cap (confirmed via search), and the absence of domestic rent/school/medical deductions (confirmed via multiple sources)
- [B] The school fee exemption is explicitly linked to the worker being a foreigner in Vietnam (or a Vietnamese posted abroad). A Vietnamese employee in Vietnam whose employer pays for their child's private school fees is taxed on that benefit; an expat employee in the same office, in the same situation, is not. This is a direct nationality-correlated differential under the same Circular. — source: https://dhplaw.vn/en/personal-income-tax-is-not-exempt-when-paying-school-fees-for-children-of-vietnamese-working-in-the-country/ (confirmed via search result snippet)
- [C] The combined value of the expat exemptions (housing cap + school fees + relocation + airfare) can represent tens of thousands of USD per year in tax savings for a senior expat on a typical expat package, compared to zero equivalent relief for a Vietnamese worker — this specific dollar-value comparison is not sourced but follows directly from the confirmed exemption values and typical expat package structures

## Question B: Mortgage Interest Deduction

### B.1 Vietnam

- [B] Vietnam's PIT system does NOT allow any deduction for mortgage interest or housing loan interest paid by an individual on their primary residence. The PwC Vietnam individual deductions summary lists no such deduction. — source: https://taxsummaries.pwc.com/vietnam/individual/deductions (site returned 403); confirmed via https://www.vietnam-briefing.com/doing-business-guide/vietnam/taxation-and-accounting/individual-income-tax search result summary which lists no mortgage interest deduction in the deductions enumeration
- [B] The 2026 amended PIT Law does not introduce a mortgage interest deduction. — source: KPMG new PIT Law summary (search result confirmed), https://kpmg.com/xx/en/our-insights/gms-flash-alert/2026/flash-alert-2026-040.html

### B.2 ASEAN countries

**Thailand:**
- [A] Thailand allows a mortgage interest deduction for interest paid on a loan to purchase or construct a residential building in Thailand, capped at THB 100,000 per year. This has been a long-standing allowance. Additionally, a temporary construction incentive (2024–2025) allows an extra deduction of THB 10,000 per THB 1M of construction costs (up to THB 100,000) for new builds completed by end-2025. — source: https://www.lexology.com/library/detail.aspx?g=cac7fcbc-fb50-4e81-8c57-10e18bc346f2 (search result confirmed citing Thailand's Revenue Department rules); https://taxsummaries.pwc.com/thailand/individual/deductions (confirmed via search result)

**Philippines:**
- [B] The Philippines does NOT allow mortgage interest, medical expenses, or other personal expenses as deductions from individual income tax. Under the TRAIN Law (2018 onwards), individual employees cannot deduct these items. Only business-related interest is deductible for self-employed/professional taxpayers. — source: https://taxsummaries.pwc.com/philippines/individual/deductions (site returned 403, confirmed via search result summary directly quoting: "home mortgage interest, medical expenses, contributions, and other personal expenses cannot be claimed as deductions for income tax purposes")

**Indonesia:**
- [B] Indonesia's PIT system does not appear to allow mortgage interest deductions for individuals. The allowable deductions for employees are limited to: occupational expenses (5% of gross, capped at IDR 500,000/month), pension contributions, and the non-taxable income threshold (PTKP). No housing loan interest deduction appears. — source: https://www.aseanbriefing.com/doing-business-guide/indonesia/taxation-and-accounting/individual-income-tax (site returned 403, confirmed via search result summary); https://taxsummaries.pwc.com/indonesia/individual/deductions (confirmed via search result)

**Singapore:**
- [B] Singapore does NOT allow mortgage interest deduction on owner-occupied residential properties. Mortgage interest is deductible only where the property generates taxable income (i.e., rental properties only). IRAS explicitly states "No deductions are allowed for medical expenses or for any other personal or household expenditure." — source: https://www.iras.gov.sg/taxes/individual-income-tax/basics-of-individual-income-tax/what-is-taxable-what-is-not/income-from-property-rented-out (search result confirmed); https://internationalservices.hsbc.com/content/dam/hsbc/hsbcis/docs/reports/tax-guides/singapore_tax_guide_faq.pdf (site returned 403, confirmed via search result)

**Malaysia:**
- [B] Malaysia introduced a new housing loan interest relief for first-time homebuyers effective YA 2025 (announced in Budget 2025 October 2024): up to RM 7,000/year for homes up to RM 500,000, or up to RM 5,000/year for homes priced RM 500,000–RM 750,000. The Sale and Purchase Agreement must be signed between 1 January 2025 and 31 December 2027, and the property must be used as the buyer's primary residence. This is a new, time-limited measure (3 years). Prior to YA 2025, Malaysia did not offer this relief for owner-occupied homes. — source: https://bernama.com/en/news.php?id=2353589 (search result confirmed); https://theedgemalaysia.com/node/730765 (search result confirmed); https://www.freemalaysiatoday.com/category/nation/2024/10/18/rm7000-tax-relief-on-first-homes-costing-up-to-rm500000/ (search result confirmed)

### B.3 Western countries

**USA:**
- [A] The USA allows mortgage interest deduction on the first USD 750,000 of mortgage debt (for loans originated after 15 December 2017, under TCJA 2017). For pre-TCJA loans, the cap was USD 1 million. The USD 750,000 cap was made permanent under the "One Big Beautiful Bill Act" (2025 tax legislation). The deduction applies only to taxpayers who itemize deductions (estimated ~13% of filers post-TCJA). — source: https://www.visaverge.com/knowledge/permanent-750000-acquisition-debt-limit-enacted-for-2026-onward-under-obbba/ (search result confirmed); https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IF13190 (search result confirmed)

**Germany:**
- [B] Germany does not allow mortgage interest deduction on owner-occupied residential properties. Mortgage interest on rental properties is deductible as a rental expense. Germany never had a broadly available owner-occupied mortgage interest deduction comparable to the US or UK. — source: https://hypofriend.de/en/are-my-mortgage-payments-tax-deductible.fmf (search result confirmed); https://www.thelocal.de/20250825/are-there-tax-benefits-to-having-a-mortgage-in-germany (search result confirmed)

**UK:**
- [B] The UK abolished Mortgage Interest Relief at Source (MIRAS) on 6 April 2000 under the Blair government. MIRAS had provided relief on the first GBP 30,000 of a mortgage since 1969; it was progressively restricted from 1994 (to basic-rate tax only, then phased down) and fully abolished in 2000. The stated rationale was that it had become a "middle-class perk" that did not materially increase homeownership rates. — source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mortgage_interest_relief_at_source (search result confirmed); https://researchbriefings.files.parliament.uk/documents/SN01536/SN01536.pdf (site returned 403, content confirmed via search result summary)

**France:**
- [B] France abolished mortgage tax relief (the "crédit d'impôt sur les intérêts d'emprunt immobilier") from January 2011. The French Finance Minister stated the credit was "too expensive and inefficient." Existing recipients at the time of abolition were grandfathered in. — source: https://www.french-property.com/news/tax_france/mortgage_tax_relief_abolished (search result confirmed)

### B.4 Policy economics argument against mortgage interest deductions

- [A] The mortgage interest deduction (MID) is regressive: it delivers the largest benefit to the highest-income homeowners who have the largest mortgages and the highest marginal tax rates. In the US, 96.8% of the MID benefit goes to households with incomes above USD 100,000, and 78.2% goes to those above USD 200,000. — source: https://nlihc.org/sites/default/files/AG-2025/6-54_The-Mortgage-Interest-Deduction.pdf (search result confirmed)
- [A] The MID inflates housing prices rather than increasing homeownership: economic research finds the MID has minimal effect on homeownership rates and instead capitalizes into housing costs, making homes less affordable for non-owners. — source: https://www.aeaweb.org/research/mortgage-interest-tax-deduction-implications-housing-prices (site returned 403, confirmed via search result summary citing AEA publication)
- [A] Fiscal cost is large with limited policy return: the US MID cost USD 25.4 billion in federal revenue in FY2024 (Joint Committee on Taxation estimate). Without TCJA caps, the cost would exceed USD 100 billion by FY2027. — source: https://nlihc.org/sites/default/files/AG-2025/6-54_The-Mortgage-Interest-Deduction.pdf (search result confirmed); https://www.crfb.org/blogs/mortgage-interest-deduction-should-be-table (search result confirmed)
- [B] In ASEAN context: countries with high homeownership rates (Singapore ~90%, Vietnam ~90%) and limited tax-filing cultures have generally not adopted MIDs. The policy benefit is also structurally limited in countries where mortgage markets are less developed and most buyers transact in cash or with shorter loan terms. — source: synthesized from ASEAN briefing search results; Singapore IRAS confirmed no MID; Indonesia confirmed no MID

## Question C: Medical Expense Deduction

### C.1 Vietnam

- [B] Vietnam's PIT does NOT allow any deduction for out-of-pocket medical expenses paid by individuals beyond the mandatory 1.5% health insurance contribution (employee share). The 1.5% health insurance contribution is deductible (as a mandatory insurance contribution), but no further medical expense deduction exists. — source: https://taxsummaries.pwc.com/vietnam/individual/deductions (confirmed via search result listing all deductions; medical expenses not among them); https://vietanlaw.com/personal-income-tax-deductions-in-2025/ (site returned 403, confirmed via search result)
- [B] Vietnam's out-of-pocket health expenditure is approximately 39.6% of total health expenditure, meaning a significant portion of healthcare costs fall directly on patients — yet no PIT relief is available for these costs. — source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8142235/ (search result confirmed)
- [B] The 2026 amended PIT Law does not introduce a medical expense deduction for individuals. — source: KPMG new PIT Law summary (search result confirmed), https://kpmg.com/xx/en/our-insights/gms-flash-alert/2026/flash-alert-2026-040.html

### C.2 ASEAN comparisons

**Thailand:**
- [B] Thailand allows deduction of health insurance premiums paid for personal health insurance policies up to THB 25,000 per year (subject to combined life + health insurance cap of THB 100,000). This is a deduction for insurance premiums, not for out-of-pocket medical expenses directly. — source: https://www.lumahealth.com/health-insurance/thailand/tax-deduction/ (site returned 403, confirmed via search result summary); https://taxsummaries.pwc.com/thailand/individual/deductions (confirmed via search result)
- [C] Whether Thailand allows a separate deduction for direct out-of-pocket medical fees (beyond insurance premiums) is not confirmed by the sources accessed. Search results focused on insurance premiums only. Needs further verification.

**Philippines:**
- [B] The Philippines does NOT allow medical expenses as personal deductions from individual income tax under TRAIN Law. — source: https://taxsummaries.pwc.com/philippines/individual/deductions (confirmed via search result directly quoting: "home mortgage interest, medical expenses, contributions, and other personal expenses cannot be claimed as deductions for income tax purposes")

**Malaysia:**
- [B] Malaysia allows tax relief on medical expenses for serious diseases for the taxpayer, spouse, and children (RM 8,000 per year), and on medical expenses for parents with disabilities or requiring treatment (RM 8,000 per year, with a RM 1,000 sub-limit for general medical checks for parents). — source: https://ringgitplus.com/en/blog/income-tax/malaysia-personal-income-tax-guide-2024-ya-2023.html (site returned 403, confirmed via search result summary); https://blog.fundingsocieties.com.my/personal-income-tax-relief-malaysia/ (search result confirmed)

**Singapore:**
- [B] Singapore does NOT allow deduction for medical expenses or any personal/household expenditure under personal income tax. — source: https://www.iras.gov.sg/taxes/individual-income-tax/basics-of-individual-income-tax/what-is-taxable-what-is-not/income-from-property-rented-out (search result confirmed)

**Indonesia:**
- [C] Indonesia's PIT deductions do not appear to include a medical expense deduction, but this was not directly confirmed by a primary source in the research conducted.

### C.3 Policy economics argument: social insurance systems and medical deductions

- [B] The standard economic argument is that where a robust mandatory social health insurance system exists, a separate PIT medical expense deduction is unnecessary because the insurance system already pools and covers most medical costs. The deduction becomes relevant only in systems where healthcare is largely out-of-pocket or voluntary. Countries with comprehensive national health systems (UK NHS, German statutory health insurance) typically do not offer PIT medical expense deductions precisely because out-of-pocket costs are already minimized by insurance. — source: https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/545121468028868365/pdf/WPS4821.pdf (World Bank Working Paper: "Social Health Insurance vs. Tax-Financed Health Systems," search result confirmed); synthesized from OECD Universal Health Coverage framework context
- [B] Vietnam's health insurance system covers approximately 91% of the population (as of 2023–2024 data), but the benefit package is limited and out-of-pocket costs remain high (~39.6% of total health expenditure). This creates a gap: the social insurance argument for not having a medical deduction is weaker in Vietnam than in countries with more comprehensive coverage. — source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8422827/ (confirmed); https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lanwpc/article/PIIS2666-6065(23)00172-4/fulltext (search result confirmed); https://www.wtwco.com/en-us/insights/2024/07/vietnam-proposal-to-increase-contributions-to-stabilize-the-public-healthcare-system (search result confirmed)
- [B] The 2024 amended Health Insurance Law (Law No. 51/2024/QH15) raised the mandatory employee contribution ceiling structure but does not add a PIT deduction for out-of-pocket spending beyond the contribution. — source: https://www.hrforte.com/taxtok-hrforte/vietnam-health-insurance-update-2025-more-benefits-same-cost-yes-really (search result confirmed)

## Open Conflicts / Gaps

1. **Housing allowance nationality scope (minor gap):** Multiple sources state the 15% housing cap applies to "all employees," but in practice it is universally discussed in expat-facing materials. Whether it could theoretically be claimed by a Vietnamese employee whose employer provides housing in a standard lease arrangement is confirmed by the legal text but not confirmed with a case example. Gap: low-stakes.

2. **Thailand medical deduction for out-of-pocket costs:** Search results confirmed insurance premium deductions (THB 25,000) but did not clearly confirm or deny a separate deduction for direct out-of-pocket medical fees. The PWC Thailand deductions page returned 403. Grade C until verified.

3. **Indonesia and Philippines mortgage/medical deductions:** Secondary confirmation only; PWC primary source pages returned 403. The conclusions (no deductions) are consistent across all accessible secondary sources but lack a primary-source fetch.

4. **School fees at "non-Vietnamese curriculum schools" specifically:** Sources confirm the exemption applies to "general education schools in Vietnam, kindergarten through high school." One source notes that "children attending international schools or foreign-invested schools do NOT qualify" — which contradicts the description in the task prompt. This conflict should be flagged: the task describes the exemption as covering "non-Vietnamese curriculum schools," but the legal text appears to require schools in Vietnam (any curriculum). The DHP Law article and VBP article both indicate the exemption covers any school in Vietnam K-12, not limited to international schools. The phrase "non-Vietnamese curriculum" in the task may reflect a common misunderstanding. Sources: search result from https://vietnam.incorp.asia/pit-in-vietnam/ note: "Foreign children attending international schools or foreign-invested schools do NOT qualify for tuition exemption" — this conflicts with other sources. This conflict needs resolution; likely the exemption covers any K-12 school in Vietnam, but the type-of-school eligibility rule varies by interpretation. Mark as [C] pending primary-source verification.

5. **Vietnam 2026 law full text:** The KPMG PDF (assets.kpmg.com) and several key Vietnam Briefing articles returned 403. All conclusions about 2026 law are confirmed via search snippets and secondary summaries, not full-text primary reads.
