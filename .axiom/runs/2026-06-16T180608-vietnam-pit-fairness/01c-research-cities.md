## Key Findings

### SECTION 1: Living Costs in HCMC and Hanoi

**1.1 [B] HCMC single-person monthly cost of living (excluding rent): ~12.2M VND/month (mid-2026 Numbeo data)**
Numbeo's crowd-sourced price database estimates monthly costs for a single person in Ho Chi Minh City excluding rent at approximately VND 12,192,401 as of May/June 2026. This covers food, local transport, utilities, and personal expenses.
Source: Numbeo HCMC Cost of Living page — https://www.numbeo.com/cost-of-living/in/Ho-Chi-Minh-City

**1.2 [B] HCMC 1-bedroom apartment rent: ~8.2M–14M VND/month depending on location**
- City-centre 1BR: approximately VND 14,000,000/month (~USD 545)
- Outside city centre 1BR: approximately VND 8,200,000/month (~USD 320)
These figures appear in multiple cost-of-living aggregators drawing from Numbeo and similar crowdsourced sources.
Source: Numbeo/Wise.com HCMC cost-of-living data — https://www.numbeo.com/cost-of-living/in/Ho-Chi-Minh-City; https://wise.com/gb/cost-of-living/vietnam/ho-chi-minh-city

**1.3 [B] HCMC total monthly budget for a single local worker (conservative): approximately 15–22M VND/month**
Combining Numbeo non-rent costs (~12.2M VND) with a non-central 1BR apartment (~8.2M VND) yields ~20.4M VND/month. A range of 15–22M VND/month is consistent across multiple cost-of-living sources for a single local worker living modestly (shared apartment or outer-district rental reduces cost; a modest but private 1BR lifestyle sits near 20M VND).
Source: Numbeo estimates above; Vietnam Airlines cost guide 2025 — https://www.vietnamairlines.com/us/en/plan-book/travel/travel-guide/cost-of-living-in-ho-chi-minh-city-vietnam; livingcost.org — https://livingcost.org/cost/vietnam/ho-chi-minh

**1.4 [B] Hanoi 1-bedroom apartment rent: approximately 10–14M VND/month (central), 7–10M VND/month (non-central)**
Hanoi's rental market is broadly comparable to HCMC, slightly lower for non-central districts. City-centre 1BR typically VND 10,000,000–14,000,000/month; outer districts VND 7,000,000–10,000,000/month.
Source: Numbeo Hanoi — https://www.numbeo.com/cost-of-living/in/Hanoi; Nomads Hanoi — https://nomads.com/cost-of-living/in/hanoi

**1.5 [B] Hanoi total monthly cost (single person) estimated ~15–22M VND/month**
Numbeo indicates Hanoi's total costs are broadly similar to HCMC, with some sources suggesting Hanoi is marginally cheaper in the outer districts. Total budget of 15–22M VND/month for a single local worker in a modest private apartment is consistent across aggregators.
Source: Numbeo comparison page HCMC vs Hanoi — https://www.numbeo.com/cost-of-living/compare_cities.jsp?country1=Vietnam&city1=Ho+Chi+Minh+City&country2=Vietnam&city2=Hanoi

**1.6 [B] Living Wage benchmark for Vietnam Region 1 (HCMC + Hanoi): ~9.3M VND/month gross (2025, single full-time worker in a 2-adult/2-child family)**
The Anker Research Institute (Global Living Wage Coalition) estimated the gross living wage for Vietnam Minimum Wage Region 1 at VND 9,294,701/month as of June 2025. This represents the gross wage needed by a typical worker in a 2-adult/2-child family (with one adult working full-time, one part-time) to afford a nutritious diet, healthy housing, healthcare, secondary education, and a small emergency margin. Note: this is a family living wage spread across earners, not a single-person total cost budget.
Source: Anker Research Institute Living Wage Report 2025 Update, Region 1 Vietnam — https://www.ankerresearchinstitute.org/vietnam-benchmarks/livingwage-nonmetroargentina-2020-emhn8-6k6ew-59zrl-bpbkr-gr7r6-x8622-met58-6kxed-g8a6z-78twb-gs5cl-c6xjj-K7AzR-tpj98-tw3t2; cited in Global Living Wage Coalition — https://www.globallivingwage.org/living-wage-benchmarks/living-wage-for-minimum-wage-region-1-vietnam/

**1.7 [B] 2022 Anker benchmark showed living wage ~80% above Region 1 minimum wage; 22.4% living wage gap for surveyed workers**
The Fair Labor Association's 2022 Vietnam Wage Trends Report found that the average monthly net wage for workers at surveyed companies showed a living wage gap of 22.4% (equivalent to VND 1,691,574 or ~USD 69.70 short per month). The Anker benchmark for Region 1 in 2022 was approximately 80% above the minimum wage set that year.
Source: Fair Labor Association, "Wage Trends Report — Vietnam, February 2024" — https://www.fairlabor.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/03/2022-Viet-Nam-Wage-Trends-Report-and-Recommendations.pdf

### SECTION 2: The Deduction Gap

**2.1 [A] PIT personal deduction is VND 11M/month nationwide through June 2026; rising to VND 15.5M/month from July 1, 2026 — and is a UNIFORM NATIONAL FIGURE, NOT differentiated by city or region**
Vietnam's personal income tax personal deduction (giảm trừ gia cảnh) is set by law and applies equally to all tax residents anywhere in Vietnam. The same VND 11,000,000/month applied whether the worker lives in HCMC, Hanoi, or a remote rural province. The new Law 109/2025/QH15 raises this to VND 15,500,000/month from July 1, 2026, again uniformly national. No provision in the PIT law or any amendment introduces regional differentiation of the personal deduction. This uniformity is confirmed across all primary and secondary tax sources reviewed.
Source: Resolution 954/2020/UBTVQH14 (setting VND 11M rate) — cited in LawNet Vietnam https://lawnet.vn/en/laws/vietnam-in-2024-what-are-the-personal-deductions-for-dependents-what-do-personal-deductions-include-138963.html; Law 109/2025/QH15 (setting VND 15.5M rate) confirmed by EY Vietnam, December 2025 — https://www.ey.com/en_vn/technical/tax/tax-and-law-updates/new-personal-income-tax-law-109-2025-qh25; Vietnam Government portal official announcement — https://en.baochinhphu.vn/viet-nam-to-raise-monthly-personal-income-tax-deductions-from-2026-111251017171305798.htm

**2.2 [A] Vietnam's minimum wage in Region I (covering HCMC and Hanoi) in 2024–2025: VND 4,960,000/month**
Under Decree 74/2024/ND-CP (effective July 1, 2024), the monthly minimum wage for Region I — covering urban and main rural districts of Hanoi, Ho Chi Minh City, Hai Phong, Dong Nai, and Binh Duong — is VND 4,960,000/month. This is the legal floor. It rises to VND 5,310,000/month under Decree 293/2025/ND-CP effective January 1, 2026.
Source: Links International wage update — https://linksinternational.com/minimum-wage-in-vietnam/; China Briefing 2026 update — https://www.china-briefing.com/china-outbound-news/vietnams-new-minimum-wage-january-1-2026

**2.3 [A] GSO 2024: national average monthly per capita income ~VND 5.4M; Southeast region (including HCMC) ~VND 7.1M; urban average ~VND 6.9M**
The General Statistics Office's 2024 Residential Living Standards Survey (preliminary results) found:
- National average: VND 5,400,000/month per capita, up 8.8% from 2023
- Southeast region (includes HCMC): VND 7,100,000/month per capita — highest of all regions
- HCMC specifically: VND 6,510,000/month per capita (fourth highest province)
- Urban areas nationally: VND 6,900,000/month per capita
- Rural areas nationally: VND 4,500,000/month per capita
Note: these are per-capita household income figures, not individual employment wages. Household income divided by family members, not worker income.
Source: Vietnam Briefing citing GSO 2024 Living Standards Survey — https://www.vietnam-briefing.com/news/vietnams-rising-purchasing-power-2024-household-living-standards-survey.html/; VietnamNet citing same survey — https://vietnamnet.vn/en/binh-duong-tops-hanoi-and-hcmc-in-vietnam-s-highest-income-provinces-ranking-2381324.html

**2.4 [B] Average monthly employment wages in HCMC: approximately VND 9.7M–10.9M/month (formal sector)**
Vietnam Briefing and other salary sources cite average monthly wages in the Southeast/HCMC at approximately VND 9.7 million (US$370) for typical formal-sector workers. State-owned enterprise workers average VND 10.91M/month; foreign-invested enterprises VND 9.28M/month; private domestic firms VND 8.10M/month. These are average wages, meaning roughly half of HCMC workers earn more, half earn less.
Source: Vietnam Briefing 2025 Wages Overview — https://www.vietnam-briefing.com/news/vietnam-wages-in-2025-overview-trends-implications.html/; Talent Net Group salary comparison — https://www.talentnetgroup.com/vn/featured-insights/socio-economic-insights/average-salary-hcmc-hanoi

**2.5 [B] The deduction gap at the median: the 11M VND deduction equals roughly 100–114% of average HCMC per-capita household income but only ~55% of actual employment wages**
- Average per-capita household income in HCMC: ~VND 6.5–7.1M/month (GSO data above)
- Average formal-sector wage in HCMC: ~VND 9.7M/month
- Personal deduction (through June 2026): VND 11M/month
At face value, the deduction (VND 11M) exceeds the average per-capita household income (VND 6.5–7.1M) — which would appear generous. However, this comparison is misleading: (a) per-capita household income averages individual shares across entire families including non-earners; (b) a single urban worker living alone in HCMC faces total living costs of ~VND 15–22M/month, meaning the deduction (VND 11M) covers only 50–73% of minimum realistic living costs before tax, leaving VND 4–11M of necessary living expenses in the taxable base.
Source: derived from facts 2.3, 2.4, 1.3 above.

**2.6 [B] No official Vietnamese government study or World Bank study specifically quantifying the deduction gap vs. urban living costs was found; but policy acknowledgment exists**
No primary government-commissioned or World Bank study was identified that explicitly calculates the deduction adequacy ratio for HCMC workers. However, the government's own justification for the 2026 reform (raising deduction from 11M to 15.5M) explicitly acknowledged that the deduction had not kept pace with "rising living costs" since 2020 — implicitly confirming the inadequacy. The government stated the increase aimed "to ease the tax burden and better reflect current living costs."
Source: Vietnam Government portal announcement — https://en.baochinhphu.vn/viet-nam-to-raise-monthly-personal-income-tax-deductions-from-2026-111251017171305798.htm; VietnamNet coverage of reform — https://vietnamnet.vn/en/millions-set-to-benefit-from-personal-income-tax-reduction-from-2026-2477049.html

**2.7 [B] The deduction was last raised in 2020 (from VND 9M to VND 11M) and remained frozen for six years despite CPI increases**
Resolution 954/2020/UBTVQH14 raised the personal deduction from VND 9,000,000 to VND 11,000,000 and the dependent deduction from VND 3,600,000 to VND 4,400,000, effective January 1, 2020. The 2026 reform is the first revision since then — a 6-year freeze during which Vietnam's CPI rose and urban wages grew by roughly 30–40%. The VND 11M deduction in 2020 was worth approximately VND 14.3–15.4M in 2026 purchasing power terms (at ~3% p.a. CPI), meaning the VND 15.5M new deduction only slightly outpaces inflation.
Source: LawNet Vietnam citing Resolution 954/2020/UBTVQH14 — https://lawnet.vn/en/laws/vietnam-in-2024-what-are-the-personal-deductions-for-dependents-what-do-personal-deductions-include-138963.html; ECOVIS Vietnam — https://ecovis.com.vn/en/personal-income-tax-deduction-in-vietnam-an-increase-in-2026/; global.ecovis.com — https://global.ecovis.com/personal-income-tax-deduction-in-vietnam-an-increase-in-2026/

### SECTION 3: Middle-Class Tax Burden

**3.1 [A] PIT bracket structure (effective through June 30, 2026 — current as of this research date)**
Progressive rates applied to monthly taxable income (gross salary minus social insurance deductions minus personal/dependent deductions):
- Up to VND 5M: 5%
- VND 5–10M: 10%
- VND 10–18M: 15%
- VND 18–32M: 20%
- VND 32–52M: 25%
- VND 52–80M: 30%
- Above VND 80M: 35%
Source: Circular 111/2013/TT-BTC, confirmed by multiple tax authorities.
Source: Vietnam Briefing doing-business guide — https://www.vietnam-briefing.com/doing-business-guide/vietnam/taxation-and-accounting/individual-income-tax; Vietnam Incorp — https://vietnam.incorp.asia/pit-in-vietnam/

**3.2 [A] New 5-bracket PIT structure (effective July 1, 2026 under Law 109/2025/QH15)**
- Up to VND 10M/month: 5%
- VND 10–30M/month: 10%
- VND 30–60M/month: 20%
- VND 60–100M/month: 30%
- Above VND 100M/month: 35%
The top rate threshold rises from VND 80M to VND 100M/month. Note that the 15% and 25% brackets are eliminated — the brackets go 5%→10%→20%, meaning earners currently in the 15% or 25% brackets move to 10% or 20% respectively. The gap from 10% to 20% (a 10-percentage-point jump) on taxable income over VND 30M is significant.
Source: VietnamNet 5-bracket report — https://vietnamnet.vn/en/vietnam-to-cut-pit-brackets-from-7-to-5-top-rate-remains-35-2424475.html; VietnamNet reform benefit story — https://vietnamnet.vn/en/millions-set-to-benefit-from-personal-income-tax-reduction-from-2026-2477049.html; Vietnam.vn (government portal) on rate reductions — https://www.vietnam.vn/en/thue-tncn-bac-15-25-giam-con-10-va-20-ai-huong-loi

**3.3 [B] Effective tax calculation for a "typical HCMC middle-class" worker earning VND 20M/month gross (current rules, no dependents)**
Step-by-step under current brackets (valid through June 2026):
1. Gross salary: VND 20,000,000
2. Minus employee social insurance (10.5%): VND 2,100,000
3. Minus personal deduction: VND 11,000,000
4. Taxable income: VND 6,900,000
5. PIT at first VND 5M (5%): VND 250,000
6. PIT on remaining VND 1.9M (10%): VND 190,000
7. Total monthly PIT: VND 440,000
8. Effective PIT rate on gross: ~2.2%
Take-home: VND 20M - VND 2.1M (SI) - VND 0.44M (PIT) = ~VND 17.46M
Note: at VND 20M gross in HCMC, the PIT burden is relatively modest due to the deduction absorbing most taxable income.
Source: B-grade (calculated using confirmed bracket structure from 3.1 and deduction from 2.1)

**3.4 [B] Effective tax calculation for VND 25M/month gross (current rules, no dependents)**
1. Gross: VND 25,000,000
2. Minus SI (10.5%): VND 2,625,000
3. Minus personal deduction: VND 11,000,000
4. Taxable income: VND 11,375,000
5. PIT: VND 5M × 5% = VND 250,000; VND 6.375M × 10% = VND 637,500
6. Total monthly PIT: VND 887,500
7. Effective PIT rate on gross: ~3.5%
Source: B-grade (same derivation)

**3.5 [B] Effective tax calculation for VND 30M/month gross (current rules, no dependents)**
1. Gross: VND 30,000,000
2. Minus SI: VND 3,150,000
3. Minus personal deduction: VND 11,000,000
4. Taxable income: VND 15,850,000
5. PIT: VND 5M × 5% = VND 250,000; VND 10M × 10% = VND 1,000,000; VND 850,000 × 15% = VND 127,500
6. Total monthly PIT: VND 1,377,500
7. Effective PIT rate on gross: ~4.6%
Source: B-grade (same derivation)

**3.6 [B] Effective total tax + SI deduction rate for VND 20–30M gross earner: 12.7%–15.6% of gross**
Adding the 10.5% employee social insurance contribution to the PIT:
- VND 20M: 2.1M SI + 0.44M PIT = 2.54M total = 12.7% of gross
- VND 25M: 2.625M SI + 0.888M PIT = 3.51M total = 14.0% of gross
- VND 30M: 3.15M SI + 1.38M PIT = 4.53M total = 15.1% of gross
This is the "total formal deduction" from gross pay for a single urban worker in the VND 20–30M range.
Source: B-grade derivation from confirmed facts 3.1, 2.1, and SI rates (confirmed at fact 1.6 in existing 01-research.md)

**3.7 [B] Key structural complaint from Vietnamese economists: at VND 30–32M/month, workers jump from 15% to 20% bracket — experts called for reinstating a 10% bracket for VND 15–30M range**
Academic economist Au Thi Nguyet Lien (Nguyen Tat Thanh University) noted that taxpayers earning around VND 30–32M/month face a bracket jump that could push marginal rates to 25%. Economist Tran Van Tung publicly urged policymakers to reinstate a 10% tax bracket for monthly incomes between VND 15M and VND 30M and to cap the top rate at 30%. Critics noted that brackets were too close together, pushing middle-income earners into higher bands after modest salary increases.
Source: VietnamNet English analysis — https://vietnamnews.vn/economy/1723744/it-feels-like-a-penalty-viet-nam-s-middle-class-struggles-under-16-year-old-tax-code.html (title confirmed via search; direct content confirmed via search result snippet); VietnamNet reform article — https://vietnamnet.vn/en/vietnam-revises-personal-income-tax-law-easing-burden-for-millions-2471255.html

**3.8 [A] PIT contributed VND 189 trillion (~USD 7.4 billion) to Vietnam's national budget in 2024 — more than 9% of total budget revenue**
This figure demonstrates the fiscal importance of PIT and provides context for reform constraints: any deduction increase or bracket widening reduces this revenue.
Source: VietnamNet (cited via search result snippet from the Vietnam News "penalty" article) — https://vietnamnews.vn/economy/1723744/it-feels-like-a-penalty-viet-nam-s-middle-class-struggles-under-16-year-old-tax-code.html

**3.9 [A] Expert quote captured in search results: Vietnam is "living in 2025 with a tax system from 2009"**
Economist Nguyen Duc Thanh stated that Vietnam's PIT system has not been fundamentally redesigned since 2009, and that "what once applied to the top 1 per cent now includes ordinary professionals, creating a system that punishes effort and discourages productivity." This is from reporting on the Vietnam News investigation article.
Source: VietnamNet via search result snippet — https://vietnamnews.vn/economy/1723744/it-feels-like-a-penalty-viet-nam-s-middle-class-struggles-under-16-year-old-tax-code.html

### SECTION 4: Regional Comparison (Vietnam vs. Thailand vs. Philippines)

**4.1 [A] Vietnam's 35% top rate kicks in at VND 80M/month gross (~USD 3,280/month) before the 2026 reform; raised to VND 100M/month (~USD 4,120/month) from July 2026**
Current (pre-July 2026): the 35% rate applies to monthly taxable income above VND 80M — which translates to a gross salary of approximately VND 80M + VND 11M deduction + VND X social insurance contributions, i.e., roughly a gross salary around VND 95–100M+ triggers the top rate. Under the new law, the taxable threshold for 35% rises to VND 100M, meaning gross salary around VND 120–130M+ before hitting 35%.
Source: VietnamNet reporting on 35% threshold debate — https://vietnamnet.vn/en/the-35-tax-rate-threshold-in-vietnam-is-too-low-experts-say-2468783.html; Retail News Asia ASEAN tax comparison — https://www.retailnews.asia/vietnams-35-top-income-tax-a-look-at-southeast-aseas-steepest-rates-and-its-impact-on-retail/

**4.2 [A] Philippines: 35% top rate applies above PHP 8,000,000/year (~USD 139,000/year at ~57.5 PHP/USD); first PHP 250,000/year (~USD 4,350/year) is tax-free**
Philippines TRAIN Law brackets (2024):
- 0% on annual income up to PHP 250,000
- 15% on PHP 250,001–400,000
- 20% on PHP 400,001–800,000
- 25% on PHP 800,001–2,000,000
- 30% on PHP 2,000,001–8,000,000
- 35% above PHP 8,000,000
The 35% rate begins at annual income of PHP 8M, equivalent to ~PHP 667,000/month (~USD 11,600/month). This is approximately 2.8–3.5x higher than the equivalent Vietnam threshold.
Source: QuickBooks Philippines tax table — https://quickbooks.intuit.com/ph/tax-brackets-and-tax-tables/; GreatDay HR Philippines TRAIN Law guide — https://greatdayhr.ph/blog/train-law-income-tax-rates/; search result confirming PHP 8M threshold — https://www.respicio.ph/commentaries/income-tax-threshold-for-monthly-salary-in-the-philippines

**4.3 [A] Thailand: 35% top rate applies above THB 5,000,000/year (~USD 140,000/year at ~36 THB/USD); personal allowance THB 60,000/year plus 50% earned income deduction (max THB 100,000)**
Thailand PIT applies progressive rates 0%–35%. The top 35% bracket applies to annual income above THB 5,000,000 (~THB 417,000/month, ~USD 11,600/month). This is broadly comparable to the Philippines threshold in USD terms, and again roughly 2.8–3.5x higher than Vietnam's current threshold.
Thailand's personal allowance is THB 60,000/year (= THB 5,000/month) plus a 50%-of-employment-income deduction capped at THB 100,000/year — a total maximum deduction of THB 160,000/year (~THB 13,333/month). At an exchange rate of ~36 THB/USD, this is ~USD 370/month, comparable to Vietnam's VND 11M (~USD 430). However, the 50% employment income deduction means middle-income Thai workers effectively deduct much more as a proportion of income.
Source: ASEAN Briefing tax comparison — https://www.aseanbriefing.com/news/comparing-tax-rates-across-asean/; Retail News Asia comparison — https://www.retailnews.asia/vietnams-35-top-income-tax-a-look-at-southeast-aseas-steepest-rates-and-its-impact-on-retail/

**4.4 [B] Key comparative finding: Vietnam's upper-middle class is hit by top-bracket tax at substantially lower absolute income than Thailand or Philippines**
The summary finding across ASEAN expert commentary: while Vietnam, Thailand, and the Philippines all share a 35% top rate, in Vietnam the threshold for this rate translates to approximately USD 4,120/month from 2026 (USD 3,280 before). In Thailand and the Philippines the threshold is approximately USD 11,600/month — roughly 2.8–3.5x higher. This means a Vietnamese professional earning USD 5,000/month faces a 35% marginal rate, while their equivalent in Bangkok or Manila would face 20–30% at that income level.
Source: Retail News Asia ASEAN comparison (confirmed via search snippet) — https://www.retailnews.asia/vietnams-35-top-income-tax-a-look-at-southeast-aseas-steepest-rates-and-its-impact-on-retail/; ASEAN Briefing — https://www.aseanbriefing.com/news/comparing-tax-rates-across-asean/

**4.5 [B] Vietnamese expert consensus: 35% threshold should be raised to VND 120–150M/month (~USD 4,900–6,100/month)**
Multiple Vietnamese economists recommend setting the 35% threshold at 20x annual GDP per capita. Vietnam's GDP per capita was approximately VND 125.5M in 2025. At 20x that figure annually, the threshold should be ~VND 2.51 billion/year (~VND 209M/month). More conservative proposals call for VND 120–150M/month. The new law's VND 100M threshold falls short of these recommendations.
Source: Vietnam.vn expert roundup — https://www.vietnam.vn/en/chuyen-gia-nen-nang-nguong-thu-nhap-chiu-thue-35-len-120-150-trieu-dong; VietnamNet experts 35% threshold debate — https://vietnamnet.vn/en/the-35-tax-rate-threshold-in-vietnam-is-too-low-experts-say-2468783.html

### SECTION 5: No Regional Differentiation of PIT Deduction — Policy Context

**5.1 [A] CONFIRMED: Vietnam's PIT personal deduction is a single, nationally uniform figure — no regional variation exists**
Research across all official sources (National Assembly resolutions, Ministry of Finance guidance, tax authority circulars, PwC/EY/KPMG Vietnam publications) confirms that the personal deduction applies equally nationwide regardless of where the taxpayer lives or works. There is no HCMC/Hanoi premium, no urban supplement, and no cost-of-living adjustment by region. The same VND 11M (rising to VND 15.5M) applies to a worker in Ho Chi Minh City and to a worker in Son La province.
Source: National Assembly Resolution 954/2020/UBTVQH14; Law 109/2025/QH15; confirmed across EY, PwC, KPMG, Acclime, Vietnam Briefing secondary summaries. No regional clause found in any source reviewed.
Specific secondary confirmations: Acclime Vietnam PIT guide — https://vietnam.acclime.com/guides/personal-income-tax/; ECOVIS Vietnam — https://ecovis.com.vn/en/personal-income-tax-deduction-in-vietnam-an-increase-in-2026/

**5.2 [B] Policy rationale for uniformity: simplicity, national legal equality, and administrative feasibility — not acknowledgment that living costs are equal**
No official source provides an explicit policy statement defending the uniformity on the grounds that living costs are equal across regions. The uniformity appears to reflect: (a) the structure of Vietnam's unitary state, where tax law is national statute; (b) administrative simplicity — regional differentiation would require ongoing regional CPI benchmarking and interagency coordination; (c) equity framing — the government has not publicly acknowledged regional cost-of-living differentiation as a desirable tax design principle. The minimum wage system does differentiate by region (Regions I–IV), but that model has not been extended to PIT.
Source: derived from structure of minimum wage legislation (Decree 74/2024/ND-CP) vs. PIT legislation (Law 04/2007/QH12 as amended); commentary in Vietnam Briefing — https://www.vietnam-briefing.com/news/vietnam-wages-in-2025-overview-trends-implications.html/

**5.3 [C] No official Vietnamese government study, and no World Bank or IMF study found, that explicitly models the distributional impact of applying a uniform PIT deduction across regions with different cost of living**
Searches did not surface any academic paper, government white paper, or multilateral institution study quantifying how much more tax a given income level pays in real (purchasing-power-adjusted) terms in HCMC versus a rural province under the current system. The OECD Economic Survey of Viet Nam 2025 was identified as a potentially relevant source but the full text was not accessible. The IMF 2024 Article IV for Vietnam was also identified but not accessible.
Sources (for existence, not content): OECD Economic Surveys: Viet Nam 2025 — https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/oecd-economic-surveys-viet-nam-2025_fb37254b-en.html; IMF Vietnam 2024 Article IV — https://www.elibrary.imf.org/view/journals/002/2024/306/article-A001-en.xml

## Best Approaches Considered

**Approach 1: Use GSO per-capita income figures as proxy for average HCMC income**
Viable, but must be noted as per-capita household income (not worker wages). GSO figures are official (A-grade) but represent averages across family members. For the deduction gap argument, employment wage data (~VND 9.7M) is more relevant than household per-capita income (~VND 6.5M).

**Approach 2: Use Numbeo data for cost-of-living benchmarks**
Numbeo is the most commonly cited crowdsourced cost-of-living database. Its HCMC data (12.2M VND/month ex-rent + 8.2–14M VND rent = 20–26M VND total) aligns broadly with Vietnam Airlines, nomads.com, and livinginvietnam.com. Confidence grade: B (aggregated crowdsourced, not a government survey).

**Approach 3: Use Anker Research Institute living wage as academic benchmark**
The Anker/Global Living Wage Coalition living wage for Region 1 (VND 9.3M gross, 2025) is the most rigorous academic benchmark available. However, it models a two-adult/two-child family with shared earning, not a single worker. It is useful for the lower bound of the discussion.

**Approach 4: Calculate effective tax rates from first principles**
The tax bracket structures are confirmed from primary sources (Law 109, Circular 111). Effective tax calculations using these confirmed brackets and deductions are B-grade (reasoned from confirmed facts), not requiring additional primary source confirmation.

## Pitfalls to Avoid

1. **Do not conflate per-capita household income (~VND 6.5M for HCMC) with individual employment wages (~VND 9.7M for HCMC workers).** These are different measures. Per-capita household income divides all household income by all members including children, elderly, and non-earners.

2. **Do not treat the living wage benchmark (~VND 9.3M) as a single-person monthly cost.** The Anker benchmark is for a family of four with 1.5 earners, not a solo worker. A single person's living costs in HCMC are higher per capita (no shared housing cost economies of scale offset by having only one income).

3. **Do not assume the new 5-bracket structure (July 2026) applies to the current analysis.** As of June 2026, the 7-bracket structure still applies. The transition date is July 1, 2026. The new structure provides some relief but does not resolve the fundamental deduction gap.

4. **Do not overstate the Thailand/Philippines comparison without acknowledging that both countries have significantly higher absolute average incomes.** The comparison of top-bracket thresholds is valid for high earners but may be less relevant for assessing the burden on the median HCMC worker.

5. **The Numbeo cost data is crowdsourced, not a government survey.** Weight it as B-grade evidence; it should be corroborated with other sources rather than cited alone.

6. **Do not state as fact that an HCMC worker faces higher real tax burden than a rural worker without acknowledgment that the analysis requires PPP adjustment.** The claim is structurally sound but the precise magnitude would require a formal model.

## Open Conflicts / Gaps

**Gap 1: No accessible primary source quantifying real cost of living for a LOCAL urban worker (as opposed to expats)**
Most cost-of-living websites (Numbeo, nomads.com, Wise) are calibrated toward international visitors or expats. A local worker's food and transport costs are typically lower (eating at pho stalls rather than restaurants, using motorbike/bus not taxi). The VND 12.2M/month Numbeo figure may overstate costs for a locally-embedded worker. The true "minimum dignified living cost" for a local HCMC worker is likely in the range of VND 10–16M/month, not the Numbeo full estimate of VND 20M+.

**Gap 2: No accessible HCMC-specific average wage (as opposed to the Southeast regional average)**
The GSO data cited (VND 9.7M) is for the "Southeast region" which includes Binh Duong and Dong Nai, both with lower wages than HCMC itself. The true HCMC average formal employment wage is likely higher — possibly VND 11–15M/month — but a city-specific figure was not found in a primary GSO publication.

**Gap 3: Vietnam's "middle class" is defined inconsistently across sources**
McKinsey defines middle class as expenditure of $11/day PPP (approximately VND 8–10M/month income). The "typical middle-class" worker posited at VND 20–30M/month is in the upper-middle-income segment, not the median. This must be stated clearly in any analysis.

**Gap 4: OECD and IMF full reports not accessed**
Both the OECD Economic Survey of Vietnam 2025 and the IMF 2024 Article IV Consultation were identified as potentially containing fiscal distributional analysis, but their full text was not accessible. They may contain relevant modeling on PIT burden by income quintile.

**Gap 5: No direct comparison found of effective tax rates on "cost-of-living adjusted" basis between Vietnam and Thailand/Philippines**
The raw bracket comparison (finding 4.4) is solid. A proper comparison of effective tax rates on purchasing-power-parity-adjusted incomes for the equivalent "middle class" in each country would strengthen the argument but was not found in accessible sources.
