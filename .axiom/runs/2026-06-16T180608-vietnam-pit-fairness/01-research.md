## Key Findings

### BATCH 1: Vietnam's Current PIT Law

**1. [A] Vietnam resident progressive PIT: 7 brackets, 5%–35% (pre-July 2026)**
Under Law on Personal Income Tax (Law 04/2007/QH12 as amended, implemented via Circular 111/2013/TT-BTC), resident taxpayers pay progressive monthly rates on salary/wage income:
- Up to VND 5 million: 5%
- VND 5–10 million: 10%
- VND 10–18 million: 15%
- VND 18–32 million: 20%
- VND 32–52 million: 25%
- VND 52–80 million: 30%
- Above VND 80 million: 35%
Source: Vietnam Teaching Jobs PIT Calculator (citing Circular 111/2013/TT-BTC) — https://vietnamteachingjobs.com/personal-income-tax-calculator/; corroborated by multiple secondary sources.

**2. [A] Non-residents pay a flat 20% on Vietnam-sourced income with NO personal or dependent deductions**
Non-tax-resident individuals pay a flat 20% withheld at source on employment income derived from Vietnam, with zero access to the personal deduction (VND 11M/month) or dependent deductions (VND 4.4M/month).
Source: Vietnam Incorp Asia PIT Guide — https://vietnam.incorp.asia/pit-in-vietnam/; PwC Worldwide Tax Summaries Vietnam — https://taxsummaries.pwc.com/vietnam/individual/taxes-on-personal-income; multiple corroborating secondary sources.

**3. [A] 183-day residency rule: the same criterion applies to Vietnamese and foreign nationals**
A person is a Vietnam tax resident if they are physically present in Vietnam for 183 or more days in a calendar year OR in any consecutive 12-month period beginning from the date of first arrival. Tax residents are taxed on worldwide income; non-residents only on Vietnam-sourced income. The rule applies equally to Vietnamese citizens and foreigners — residency is determined by physical presence and registered address, not nationality.
Source: Vietnam Incorp Asia PIT Guide — https://vietnam.incorp.asia/pit-in-vietnam/; Grant Thornton Expatriate Tax Vietnam — https://www.grantthornton.global/en/insights/articles/expatriate-tax-Vietnam/

**4. [A] Personal deduction VND 11M/month; dependent deduction VND 4.4M/month per dependent (applicable through June 2026)**
Every resident taxpayer gets a monthly personal deduction of VND 11,000,000 (~USD 430 at mid-2025 rates). Registered dependents (children under 18, disabled adult children, spouses or parents unable to work with income ≤ VND 1M/month) reduce taxable income by a further VND 4,400,000/month each.
Source: LawNet Vietnam (citing Resolution 954/2020/UBTVQH14) — https://lawnet.vn/en/laws/vietnam-in-2024-what-are-the-personal-deductions-for-dependents-what-do-personal-deductions-include-138963.html

**5. [A] New PIT Law 109/2025/QH15 passed December 10, 2025; effective July 1, 2026**
The National Assembly approved Law 109/2025/QH15 on December 10, 2025. Key structural changes effective from July 1, 2026:
- Brackets reduced from 7 to 5: 5% (≤ VND 10M), 10% (VND 10–30M), 20% (VND 30–60M), 30% (VND 60–100M), 35% (> VND 100M)
- Personal deduction increased: VND 11M → VND 15.5M/month
- Dependent deduction increased: VND 4.4M → VND 6.2M/month
- Non-resident flat 20% rate: UNCHANGED
- Night-shift/overtime wages now fully exempt
Source: EY Vietnam Tax Alert, December 2025 — https://www.ey.com/en_vn/technical/tax/tax-and-law-updates/new-personal-income-tax-law-109-2025-qh25; Vietnam Briefing — https://www.vietnam-briefing.com/news/vietnams-personal-income-tax-law.html/; VietnamNet English — https://vietnamnet.vn/en/vietnam-to-cut-pit-brackets-from-7-to-5-top-rate-remains-35-2424475.html

**6. [A] Employee social insurance (SI) contributions: 10.5% of salary, fully deductible for PIT**
Vietnamese employees (and foreign employees subject to mandatory SI) contribute:
- Social Insurance: 8% of salary
- Health Insurance: 1.5% of salary
- Unemployment Insurance: 1% of salary
Total: 10.5%, all deductible from gross income before PIT is calculated.
Employer contributions add 17.5%, for a combined 28% payroll tax rate.
Source: Viet An Law (citing Decree on Social Insurance) — https://vietanlaw.com/vietnam-employee-social-insurance-contribution-rate-2025/; Vietnam Incorp Asia — https://vietnam.incorp.asia/pit-in-vietnam/

**7. [A] Foreign workers mandatory SI contribution: same rates as Vietnamese workers since 2022, with narrow exemptions**
Since December 1, 2022 (Decree 143/2021/ND-CP), foreign employees with work permits and labor contracts ≥ 12 months are subject to the same compulsory SI contributions (8% employee, 17.5% employer) as Vietnamese workers. Exemptions apply for: intra-company transferees, employees at retirement age at time of signing contract, and workers covered by bilateral social security agreements (e.g., Vietnam-South Korea, effective January 1, 2024).
Source: DNP Law — https://www.dnp-law.com/compulsory-social-insurance-contributions-and-benefits-for-foreign-employees-in-vietnam-in-2025/; KPMG Vietnam-South Korea SI Agreement — https://kpmg.com/vn/en/home/insights/2024/04/implementing-provisions-of-social-insurance-agreement-vietnam-and-south-korea.html

**8. [B] CPI-trigger mechanism for adjusting PIT deductions: 20% CPI rise required — criticized as too slow**
Under the current law, the National Assembly Standing Committee can only revise PIT deductions when the Consumer Price Index (CPI) rises by more than 20% cumulatively. This threshold can require more than 5 years to be met, while wages and living costs change more rapidly. The personal deduction was last revised in 2020 (from VND 9M to VND 11M), and the VND 11M figure had already become outdated relative to rising wages before the 2025 law replaced it.
Source: ASEAN Briefing citing PIT reform discussions — https://www.vietnam-briefing.com/news/vietnams-personal-income-tax-law.html/; VietnamNet — https://vietnamnet.vn/en/millions-set-to-benefit-from-personal-income-tax-reduction-from-2026-2477049.html

### BATCH 2: ASEAN/Regional Comparison

**9. [A] Thailand PIT: 8 brackets, 0%–35%, top rate on income > THB 5 million/year**
Thailand taxes resident income progressively:
- 0%: 0–150,000 THB
- 5%: 150,001–300,000 THB
- 10%: 300,001–500,000 THB
- 15%: 500,001–750,000 THB
- 20%: 750,001–1,000,000 THB
- 25%: 1,000,001–2,000,000 THB
- 30%: 2,000,001–5,000,000 THB
- 35%: above 5,000,000 THB
Top marginal rate: 35%. Personal allowance: THB 60,000 personal deduction + 50% employment income deduction (capped at THB 100,000). Non-residents also taxed progressively (not flat rate) on Thailand-source income.
Source: PwC Thai Tax Booklet 2024-25 — https://www.pwc.com/th/en/tax/assets/thai-tax/thai-tax-booklet-2024-25.pdf; Statrys Thailand PIT Guide — https://statrys.com/guides/thailand/tax-system-and-rates/personal-income-tax

**10. [A] Philippines PIT: 6 brackets, 0%–35%, top rate on income > PHP 8 million/year (TRAIN Law)**
Under the Tax Reform for Acceleration and Inclusion (TRAIN) Act, effective 2023 onward:
- 0%: 0–PHP 250,000
- 15%: PHP 250,001–400,000 (of excess over 250K)
- 20%: PHP 400,001–800,000
- 25%: PHP 800,001–2,000,000
- 30%: PHP 2,000,001–8,000,000
- 35%: above PHP 8,000,000
Key point: First PHP 250,000 is fully exempt (no tax). This exemption is equivalent to approximately VND 110M/year at current rates, versus Vietnam's personal deduction of VND 132M/year (11M × 12).
Source: Pinoy Benefits TRAIN Law 2024 — https://pinoybenefits.com/rates/tax/2024/; GreatDay HR Philippines — https://greatdayhr.ph/blog/income-tax-table-philippines/

**11. [A] Indonesia PIT: 5 brackets, 5%–35%, top rate on income > IDR 5 billion/year**
Under Harmonization of Tax Regulations Law (UU No. 7/2021), 2024 brackets:
- 5%: up to IDR 60 million
- 15%: IDR 60–250 million
- 25%: IDR 250–500 million
- 30%: IDR 500 million–5 billion
- 35%: above IDR 5 billion
Personal exemption (PTKP): IDR 54 million/year for a single person (IDR 4.5M/month), plus IDR 4.5M/year per dependent. Non-residents taxed at a flat 20% on Indonesia-sourced income.
Source: Invest in Asia — https://investinasia.id/blog/tax-bracket-indonesia/; Asanify PTKP explanation — https://asanify.com/glossary/ptkp/; Legal Indonesia 2024 PIT changes — https://legalindonesia.id/changes-in-the-personal-income-tax-rate-pit-pph21-in-2024-effective-rate-and-calculation-example/

**12. [A] Singapore PIT: 11 brackets, 0%–24%, top rate on income > SGD 1 million/year**
Singapore's progressive rates for tax residents (Year of Assessment 2024 onward):
- 0%: 0–SGD 20,000
- Rates rise progressively through 2%, 3.5%, 7%, 11.5%, 15%, 18%, 19%, 19.5%, 20%, 22% at SGD 320,000
- 23%: SGD 500,001–1,000,000
- 24%: above SGD 1,000,000
Top marginal rate: 24% (raised from 22% in YA 2024). Non-residents: taxed at 15% flat OR progressive resident rates, whichever is higher. Singapore does not have mandatory social insurance in the same sense — the CPF (Central Provident Fund) is a savings/pension scheme, not a traditional social insurance contribution, and is only mandatory for Singapore citizens/PRs, not most foreign workers.
Source: IRAS Singapore official — https://www.iras.gov.sg/taxes/individual-income-tax/basics-of-individual-income-tax/tax-residency-and-tax-rates/individual-income-tax-rates; SingSaver 2024 guide — https://www.singsaver.com.sg/banking/blog/personal-income-tax-in-singapore-guide

**13. [A] South Korea PIT: 8 brackets, 6%–45%, plus 10% local income surtax; effective top rate ~49.5%**
South Korea 2024 brackets (KRW annual):
- 6%: up to KRW 12 million
- 15%: KRW 12–46 million
- 24%: KRW 46–88 million
- 35%: KRW 88–150 million
- 38%: KRW 150–300 million
- 40%: KRW 300–500 million
- 42%: KRW 500 million–1 billion
- 45%: above KRW 1 billion
A local income surtax of 10% of the national tax applies, bringing the effective top marginal rate to ~49.5%. Foreign workers may elect a flat 19% rate on employment income (no deductions) for their first 20 years of employment in Korea.
Source: Korean Tax Expert — https://koreantaxexpert.com/2024/08/01/2024-income-tax-rates-in-korea-key-points-for-foreign-workers/; Tax Foundation Korea — https://taxfoundation.org/location/korea/

### BATCH 3: Fairness Analysis and Foreign Worker Angle

**14. [B] Non-resident foreign workers in Vietnam face structurally higher effective tax rates than resident Vietnamese at mid-level incomes**
A non-resident earns VND 30 million/month from Vietnam: pays VND 6 million (20% × 30M, no deduction). A resident with the same gross salary and no dependents: pays tax on VND 19M (= 30M − 11M deduction): approx. VND 2.15M at progressive rates. At VND 50M/month: non-resident pays VND 10M; resident (no dependent) pays on VND 39M: ~VND 6.35M. The 20% flat rate is only "cheaper" than the resident progressive rate at very high incomes (generally above ~VND 65M/month with no dependents), where the progressive resident rate also approaches or exceeds 20% on marginal income.
Source: Derived from bracket/deduction data confirmed above; Incorp Asia PIT Guide — https://vietnam.incorp.asia/pit-in-vietnam/; Vietnam Incorp analysis noting: "Tax residency status creates a difference of up to VND 4.75 million per month in PIT on the same VND 50 million salary."

**15. [B] Non-resident foreigners cannot access ANY deductions; resident foreigners and Vietnamese can — structuring matters enormously**
The deduction asymmetry is the key differential, not the headline rate. Vietnam's non-resident rule (flat 20%, no deductions) is the same structure used by Indonesia (flat 20%), but different from Thailand (which taxes non-residents under the same progressive schedule as residents) and Singapore (which gives non-residents at least the higher-of-15%-or-progressive test). This means Vietnam's treatment of short-stay foreign workers is more restrictive in deduction access than Thailand or Singapore.
Source: Russin & Vecchi Vietnam Tax Calculator for Foreigners — https://www.russinvecchi.com.vn/publication/vietnam-tax-calculator-for-foreigners/; Vietnam Incorp Asia — https://vietnam.incorp.asia/pit-in-vietnam/; Forvis Mazars Thailand — https://www.forvismazars.com/th/en/insights/doing-business-in-thailand/payroll/personal-income-tax

**16. [B] Vietnam's deduction threshold (VND 11M/month, ~USD 430) is low relative to average wages and compared to peers**
Vietnam's average monthly wage was ~VND 7.7 million in 2024 — the personal deduction of VND 11M thus exceeds the average wage, suggesting many low-wage workers avoid PIT entirely. However, the brackets above 11M kick in steeply (starting at 5% immediately on income above the deduction), and a worker earning VND 20M/month (~USD 785) already pays 15% on the VND 9M above the personal deduction. By contrast, Indonesia's PTKP exempts the first IDR 54M/year (IDR 4.5M/month, ~USD 280), which is lower in absolute terms but Vietnam's starting bracket at 5% immediately after the exemption bites harder than Indonesia's 5% bottom bracket which also starts low. The Philippines' PHP 250,000/year (~USD 4,300) flat exemption provides a more generous floor for low earners in that economy.
Source: Vietnam Briefing wages data — https://www.vietnam-briefing.com/news/vietnam-wages-in-2025-overview-trends-implications.html/; ASEAN Briefing PIT comparison — https://www.aseanbriefing.com/news/comparing-tax-rates-across-asean/

**17. [B] Vietnam's top 35% marginal rate kicks in at a relatively low absolute income threshold compared to peers**
Under the pre-2026 law, the 35% top rate applied to monthly income above VND 80 million (~USD 3,100/month). Under the new 2026 law, the threshold rises to VND 100 million (~USD 3,900/month). For comparison, Thailand's 35% top rate applies only above THB 5 million/year (~USD 11,800/year = ~USD 985/month, but Thailand's deduction and 50%-employment-income deduction structure means the effective threshold is much higher). The Philippines' 35% applies only above PHP 8 million/year (~USD 138,000/year). This suggests Vietnam's top rate is reached at a lower absolute income level than Philippines or Thailand, though meaningful comparison requires modeling effective rates accounting for deduction differences.
Source: Derived from bracket data in facts 1, 9, 10, 11 above; ASEAN Briefing — https://www.aseanbriefing.com/news/comparing-tax-rates-across-asean/; VietnamNet — https://vietnamnet.vn/en/vietnam-to-cut-pit-brackets-from-7-to-5-top-rate-remains-35-2424475.html

**18. [B] Vietnam's PIT as share of GDP: ~1.7% (2018 World Bank data); IMF 2024 notes PIT collection lags peers due to informality**
A 2018 World Bank Fiscal Incidence Analysis for Vietnam found that PIT accounts for approximately 9% of tax revenues and ~1.7% of GDP. The IMF 2024 Article IV Consultation report for Vietnam noted that "despite a relatively higher personal income tax rate, tax collections lag peers owing to high informality and a limited tax base." Overall tax-to-GDP ratio was 16.8% in 2023 (OECD). In the ASEAN-8 grouping in 2023, PIT averaged 12.7% of total tax revenues; Vietnam's share is lower than this average.
Sources:
- World Bank 2018 Fiscal Incidence Analysis — https://openknowledge.worldbank.org/server/api/core/bitstreams/82d58182-9fe5-4e23-be56-8bafed36ef8e/content
- IMF 2024 Article IV Consultation (Vietnam) — https://www.elibrary.imf.org/view/journals/002/2024/306/article-A001-en.xml
- OECD Revenue Statistics Asia-Pacific 2025 (Vietnam country note) — https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2025/06/revenue-statistics-in-asia-and-the-pacific-2025-country-notes_0a069779/viet-nam_3f721dfb/135ae083-en.pdf

**19. [B] Vietnam's PIT applies equally to Vietnamese nationals and resident foreigners — no nationality-based differential in rates or deductions for residents**
Vietnam's PIT law does not distinguish by nationality for tax residents: both Vietnamese citizens and foreigners who meet the 183-day residency test are subject to the identical progressive rate schedule and identical personal/dependent deductions. The relevant differential is solely residency status (resident vs. non-resident), not nationality. A Vietnamese citizen working abroad for most of the year and earning only Vietnam-source income would also be taxed at the 20% non-resident flat rate.
Source: Vietnam Incorp Asia PIT Guide — https://vietnam.incorp.asia/pit-in-vietnam/; Grant Thornton Expatriate Tax Vietnam — https://www.grantthornton.global/en/insights/articles/expatriate-tax-Vietnam/

**20. [B] Common expat-specific exemptions exist in Vietnam but are structural reliefs, not nationality-based preferential rates**
Vietnam law provides that certain foreign-worker-specific allowances are excluded from taxable income for PIT purposes, including: (a) one-time relocation allowance, (b) annual home-leave airfare, (c) school fees for expat children (at non-Vietnamese-curriculum schools up to high school level), (d) housing benefits up to 15% of total other taxable income. These exemptions are available to all workers whose employers provide these benefits, regardless of nationality, but in practice almost exclusively benefit foreign workers because they reflect the structure of multinational employer compensation packages.
Source: Vietnam Incorp Asia PIT Guide — https://vietnam.incorp.asia/pit-in-vietnam/; Russin & Vecchi — https://www.russinvecchi.com.vn/publication/vietnam-tax-calculator-for-foreigners/

## Best Approaches Considered

For the fairness analysis, two frameworks are viable:

**A. Structural/comparative approach** — compare bracket design, deduction levels, effective rate modeling at standardized income points (e.g., 1x/3x/5x average wage). Strong for cross-country comparison. Requires modeling, not just citing statutory rates. All major comparative PIT studies (World Bank, IMF, OECD) use this approach.

**B. Incidence/distributional approach** — analyze who actually pays PIT by income decile, using household survey data. Requires the World Bank 2018 Fiscal Incidence Analysis as a primary data source. More powerful for the "fairness" claim but data is 2018 and may be outdated.

**Recommended approach for the fairness essay**: Use structural/comparative (Approach A), incorporating OECD/IMF/World Bank findings as supporting evidence. Model effective rates at 1×, 2×, 5× average monthly wage (~VND 7.7M, VND 15.4M, VND 38.5M) for Vietnam vs. peers. Use the resident/non-resident differential as the core "unequal treatment" argument.

## Pitfalls to Avoid

1. **Confusing statutory top rate with effective rate**: Vietnam's 35% top rate is the same as Thailand and Philippines, but effective rates at given income levels differ significantly due to deduction design and bracket progression. Do not simply compare top rates.

2. **Claiming nationality-based differential when it is residency-based**: Vietnam's law does not discriminate by nationality. The differential is between residents and non-residents. Framing this as "Vietnamese vs. foreigners" is imprecise; it should be "residents vs. non-residents," which disproportionately affects short-stay foreign workers.

3. **Using outdated deduction figures**: The VND 11M/4.4M figures are the pre-July 2026 amounts. The new law (109/2025/QH15, effective July 1, 2026) raises these to VND 15.5M/6.2M. Any fairness analysis covering the current moment (June 2026) should note the transition is imminent.

4. **Treating the 2018 World Bank data as current**: The fiscal incidence data is from 2018. Wages, informality, and enforcement have all changed since then. Label any citation of the 1.7% PIT/GDP figure as potentially outdated.

5. **Mischaracterizing Singapore's top rate**: Singapore's 24% top rate applies only above SGD 1 million — a threshold almost no one reaches — and the overall effective rates in Singapore are low. Comparing Vietnam's 35% top rate to Singapore's 24% without noting the vastly different income thresholds and the absence of Singapore social insurance for foreigners gives a misleading picture.

6. **Ignoring social insurance interaction**: Vietnam's true labor cost wedge is PIT + 10.5% SI = potentially 45.5% at top marginal rate. This is relevant to competitiveness analysis but should be separated from the fairness-of-PIT-structure question.

## Open Conflicts / Gaps

1. **Exact old 7-bracket table**: Multiple secondary sources confirm the bracket thresholds (5M/10M/18M/32M/52M/80M VND), but the primary text (Circular 111/2013/TT-BTC) was not directly read due to site-blocking. Confidence is B, not A. The writer should note the statutory basis is Circular 111/2013 and Law 04/2007 without claiming to have read the original Vietnamese text.

2. **PIT as % of GDP — current figure**: The 1.7% figure is from the 2018 World Bank study. The OECD 2025 Vietnam country note PDF was blocked. The tax-to-GDP ratio of 16.8% in 2023 is confirmed by OECD data, but the PIT-specific share for 2023 was not extractable. Gap: writer should present the 1.7% figure as 2018 data and note the OECD 2025 country note as a source to cite for updated data.

3. **Effective rate modeling**: No source provided a direct side-by-side effective rate model at standardized income points for Vietnam vs. all five peer countries. The analysis in the fairness report will need to build this from the bracket tables confirmed above. All bracket data has been sourced; the modeled comparison is B-grade reasoning from A-grade inputs.

4. **Thailand non-resident treatment detail**: Thailand taxes non-residents under the progressive resident schedule (not a flat rate), which is more favorable than Vietnam's flat 20% for mid-income earners, but the specific deductions available to Thai non-residents vs. residents were not confirmed from a primary source in this research pass.

5. **Vietnam's PIT law text in English**: The primary legislative text (Law 04/2007/QH12 and its amendments) exists in Vietnamese. English translations used in this research are from secondary/advisory sources (PwC, EY, KPMG, Vietnam Briefing), all reputable but not official. The EY and KPMG citations to Law 109/2025/QH15 are strong secondary sources.
